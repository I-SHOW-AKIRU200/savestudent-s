# The CJP Student Protests and the 20 July 2026 Police Crackdown in Delhi

*A documentation of events, claims, and disputes, compiled 22 July 2026*

> **Note on sourcing:** This is an active, fast-moving, and contested news story. Claims from protest organizers and claims from Delhi Police directly conflict on several points (see Section 5). Where sources disagree, both versions are presented rather than one being presented as settled fact.

---

## 1. Background

- The protests are led by the **Cockroach Janta Party (CJP)**, a satirical student movement founded in May 2026 by activist **Abhijeet Dipke**. It began as an online campaign and grew into a mass protest movement after gaining a large social media following.
- The name references a remark by India's Chief Justice comparing protesting students to cockroaches — the movement adopted the insult as its mascot and identity.
- The core grievance: alleged **irregularities in the NEET-UG 2026 exam** (India's national medical entrance test), including claims of question paper leaks and organized cheating networks, plus similar allegations about **UGC-NET** and **CSIR-NET** exams.
- The central demand: **resignation of Union Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan**, whom protesters hold responsible for the exam failures.
- In the lead-up to 20 July, CJP-aligned activist **Sonam Wangchuk** had been on a 20-day hunger strike in support of the movement; he was forcibly taken to hospital on 18 July, ending the strike.

## 2. Timeline of 20 July 2026

| Time / Sequence | Event |
|---|---|
| Prior days | Delhi Police denied permission for a march from Jantar Mantar to Parliament, citing security concerns since Parliament was in session. |
| Before the march | Police blocked key roads, deployed heavy security, suspended mobile internet in parts of central Delhi, and closed several Delhi Metro stations. |
| Morning | Prohibitory orders under **Section 163 of the Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS)** — the provision that replaced the old CrPC Section 144 — were in force, barring public assembly. |
| Late morning | Thousands of protesters, mostly students, gathered at Jantar Mantar for the planned **"Sansad Chalo" (March to Parliament)** procession, despite the ban. |
| As march began | Protesters attempted to move past barricades toward Parliament Street and Connaught Place. |
| Police response | Officers used **lathi charges (baton charges)** and **tear gas** to disperse the crowd. |
| Aftermath | Parliament Street and Connaught Place were left with scattered debris — overturned barricades, broken glasses, torn clothing, and reported blood stains — described by witnesses as the visible aftermath of the clash. |

This was reported as **the largest protest in India's capital in recent years**, and was deliberately timed to coincide with the opening of the Parliament session.

## 3. Scale of Casualties and Detentions (as officially reported)

According to Delhi Police:
- **~180 total injuries** — around **60 protesters** and **118 police/security personnel**.
- **70 protesters detained**, with police stating legal action would be pursued against them.
- **5 FIRs (First Information Reports)** registered over alleged violence, stone-pelting, and vandalism.
- Police said they were examining **over 250 videos** to identify individuals involved in the clashes.

According to CJP organizers:
- Founder Abhijeet Dipke said **150 protesters** were wounded by police tear gas and baton charges.
- Organizers alleged **hundreds** were detained, a figure far above the police's stated number of 70.

## 4. Political and Institutional Reactions

- **Rahul Gandhi** (Leader of the Opposition, Congress) camped outside PM Modi's residence the following afternoon with other MPs, giving a public call to assemble in solidarity with the protesters.
- The **NCP (Sharadchandra Pawar faction)** extended support to the demand for Pradhan's resignation.
- **Union Health Minister J.P. Nadda** visited RML Hospital to meet injured protesters and separately met a CJP delegation that submitted its demands.
- Analysts cited by Al Jazeera noted that, regardless of whether the protests succeed in extracting concessions, the crackdown feeds an existing opposition narrative that the Modi government has weakened democratic checks and accountability — while the protest movement itself faces a steep climb to force any policy change.

## 5. The Central Dispute: Were the 20–21 July Police Actions Unlawful?

This is the most contested part of the story, and the claims on each side directly conflict.

### Protesters' and organizers' allegations
- CJP spokesperson **Saurav Das** alleged police used **"brute, borderline-lethal force"** against demonstrators.
- Das alleged **hundreds of peaceful protesters** were detained after the clashes.
- He claimed **lawyers were denied access** to those in detention, and that police had **not provided a complete list of detainees** — a claim that, if true, would raise due-process concerns, since Indian law requires produced-before-magistrate procedures and access to counsel for anyone detained.
- Organizers announced a sit-in ("dharna") outside Delhi Police Headquarters on the evening of 21 July over these allegations.
- Multiple videos circulated showing police striking protesters, including some already on the ground; organizers say no footage has emerged showing violence initiated by marchers.
- Some protesters reportedly required hospital treatment for head and leg injuries.

### Delhi Police's response
- Delhi Police issued a public **"fact-check"** on X (Twitter), stating: **"Claims circulating on social media regarding persons being detained and denied access to lawyers are misleading and incorrect. It is clarified that no person is currently under detention."**
- Police maintained the restrictions were lawful, aimed at preventing unauthorized entry into high-security zones around Parliament while it was in session, under a valid Section 163 BNSS prohibitory order.
- Police urged the public not to circulate "unverified content."

### Resolution (as of 21 July evening)
- Shortly before the planned dharna at Police HQ, CJP announced that its **lawyers confirmed all detainees had been released**, and called off the sit-in. Saurav Das posted: *"Our lawyers have just told us that ALL the detainees have been released… EVERYONE IS REQUESTED TO MAINTAIN PEACE."*
- This leaves an unresolved factual gap: police said **no one was under detention**, while CJP said detainees existed and were **released** only after their lawyers intervened. Independent verification of what happened during the intervening hours has not been reported.

### Independent fact-checking note
Fact-checkers flagged that several viral videos shared as footage from 20 July were actually **recycled from unrelated, earlier protests**, and cautioned the public to rely on verified reporting rather than isolated social media posts.

## 6. Legal Context: Section 163 BNSS (formerly Section 144 CrPC)

- Section 163 of the BNSS (the successor to the old CrPC Section 144) allows a magistrate/police to prohibit assembly of persons in an area to prevent apprehended danger to public order.
- This provision has a well-documented history of being used against protest movements across party lines in Delhi — including past crackdowns on AAP workers, wrestlers' protests at Jantar Mantar, and TMC protests outside the Election Commission — and has also faced court scrutiny in the past over whether its invocation was justified in specific cases.
- Whether its use on 20 July 2026 was a proportionate, lawful measure or an overreach used to suppress dissent is precisely the point of dispute between organizers and the government; no court ruling on this specific instance had been reported as of 22 July 2026.

## 7. Key Open Questions (Unresolved as of this writing)

1. Exact number of people detained on 20 July — police say the total detained was 70 (with legal action to follow), while organizers claimed a much higher, unspecified number were held **and** implied lawyers were initially blocked from meeting them — a claim police deny happened at all.
2. Whether any independent investigation into police conduct (as demanded by organizers) will be initiated.
3. Whether any of the 5 FIRs will lead to prosecutions, and against whom.
4. Whether Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan will face any government action or resign.
5. What happens next — CJP has stated it will **not hold further street marches** in Delhi due to safety concerns after this crackdown, while continuing to press its demands through other means.
